## node: big-five
title: Big Five (OCEAN)
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Trait model

Five broad, continuous dimensions describe most of the stable variation in human personality: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism. Each trait splits into two facets (DeYoung), giving the model a clean two-level hierarchy.

### Structure
- The most replicated structure in psychology — recovered across languages, cultures, and instruments
- Traits are continuous dimensions, not types; everyone sits somewhere on all five
- Each trait divides into two correlated but distinct facets (10 facets total)
- Scores are descriptive tendencies, not verdicts — they say how you tend to respond, not who you are allowed to be

### Note
Trait scores describe averages across situations. Behavior in any single moment is driven by context at least as much as by trait.

### Bridges
- [hexaco] HEXACO extends the model with a sixth dimension, Honesty-Humility.
- [attachment] Trait-level anxiety (Neuroticism) relates to attachment anxiety in relationships.
- [self-concept] Traits become incorporated into self-schemas — the vocabulary you use about yourself.

### Sources
- Goldberg (1993). The structure of phenotypic personality traits.
- DeYoung, Quilty & Peterson (2007). Between facets and domains: 10 aspects of the Big Five.
- Soto & John (2017). The next Big Five Inventory (BFI-2).

## node: openness
title: Openness
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Trait

Appreciation for ideas, aesthetics, curiosity, and breadth of experience. High scorers seek novelty and pattern; low scorers prefer the familiar and concrete.

### Structure
- Facets: Intellect (ideas) and Aesthetic Openness (art, beauty, experience)
- Predicts engagement with art, science, and unconventional viewpoints
- The two facets can diverge — an engineer high in Intellect may be low in Aesthetic Openness, and vice versa

### Bridges
- [intellect] Engagement with abstract ideas and problem-solving.
- [aesthetic-openness] Sensitivity to beauty, art, and inner experience.

### Sources
- DeYoung, Quilty & Peterson (2007). Between facets and domains.

## node: conscientiousness
title: Conscientiousness
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Trait

The tendency to be organized, dependable, and persistent in goal pursuit. The strongest trait predictor of academic and job performance after cognitive ability.

### Structure
- Facets: Industriousness (persistent effort) and Orderliness (structure, routine)
- Reliably predicts health behaviors, longevity, and follow-through
- What pop culture calls "grit" is psychometrically this trait's perseverance component

### Bridges
- [industriousness] Persistent effort and follow-through.
- [orderliness] Preference for structure and routine.
- [goal-setting] The trait basis of sustained, persistent goal pursuit.
- [self-efficacy] Trait foundation of agentic action — belief plus disposition.
- [grit] Grit is statistically indistinguishable from this trait's perseverance facet.

### Sources
- Roberts et al. (2007). The power of personality.
- Credé, Tynan & Harms (2017). Much ado about grit.

## node: extraversion
title: Extraversion
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Trait

Energy, positive affect, sociability, and drive toward the external world. More about reward sensitivity than mere talkativeness.

### Structure
- Facets: Enthusiasm (warmth, positive affect) and Assertiveness (dominance, drive)
- High scorers experience more frequent positive emotion — the trait most tied to positive affect
- Warmth and dominance are separable: a warm non-assertive person and a driven cold one are both "extraverted" in different ways

### Bridges
- [enthusiasm] Warmth, sociability, and positive affect.
- [assertiveness] Dominance, drive, and leadership emergence.

### Sources
- Depue & Collins (1999). Neurobiology of the structure of personality.

## node: agreeableness
title: Agreeableness
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Trait

A compassionate, cooperative, and conflict-sensitive interpersonal style. Distinguishes care for others from respect for norms.

### Structure
- Facets: Compassion (concern for others' wellbeing) and Politeness (respect for norms)
- Low agreeableness maps to friction, antagonism, and exploitative styles
- The Dark Triad traits are largely the low pole of this dimension plus low Honesty-Humility

### Bridges
- [compassion] Emotional concern for others' wellbeing.
- [politeness] Respect for norms and conflict avoidance.
- [dark-triad] Dark traits are largely low Agreeableness and low Honesty-Humility, not a separate species.

### Sources
- Graziano & Tobin (2002). Agreeableness: dimension of personality or social desirability?

## node: neuroticism
title: Neuroticism
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Trait

Sensitivity to threat, stress, and negative emotion. High scores mean stronger and more frequent negative emotional responses — reactivity, not dysfunction.

### Structure
- Facets: Withdrawal (anxiety, risk-aversion) and Volatility (moodiness, reactivity)
- The single strongest trait predictor of emotional-health outcomes
- High Neuroticism is common and workable — regulation skills buffer trait effects

### Note
High values do not imply dysfunction, only reactivity. The trait sets the baseline; regulation skills set the ceiling.

### Bridges
- [withdrawal] Anxiety, risk-aversion, and disengagement.
- [volatility] Moodiness and emotional reactivity.
- [emotion-regulation] High Neuroticism predicts maladaptive regulation strategies — skills can buffer the trait.
- [appraisal] High Neuroticism produces more frequent threat appraisals of the same events.
- [attachment] Trait anxiety amplifies attachment anxiety.

### Sources
- Lahey (2009). Public health significance of neuroticism.
- Barlow et al. (2014). The nature, diagnosis, and treatment of neuroticism.

## node: intellect
title: Intellect
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Facet

The Openness facet oriented toward ideas: abstract reasoning, intellectual engagement, and enjoyment of complex problems.

### Structure
- Correlates with (but is distinct from) measured intelligence
- Predicts preference for debate, theory, and conceptual play
- Pairs with Aesthetic Openness under the Openness trait

### Sources
- DeYoung, Quilty & Peterson (2007). Between facets and domains.

## node: aesthetic-openness
title: Aesthetic Openness
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Facet

The Openness facet oriented toward experience: sensitivity to beauty, art, fantasy, and emotional depth.

### Structure
- Predicts absorption in music, art, and nature
- Linked to fantasy proneness and rich inner experience
- Can be high in people with little interest in abstract argument — the facets are separable

### Sources
- DeYoung, Quilty & Peterson (2007). Between facets and domains.

## node: industriousness
title: Industriousness
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Facet

The Conscientiousness facet behind persistent effort: working hard, finishing what you start, and resisting distraction.

### Structure
- The facet that actually carries the "grit" construct
- Predicts achievement outcomes more strongly than Orderliness
- Low industriousness with high standards is the classic procrastination profile

### Bridges
- [goal-setting] Persistence is a core mediator of the goal–performance link.

### Sources
- Credé, Tynan & Harms (2017). Much ado about grit.

## node: orderliness
title: Orderliness
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Facet

The Conscientiousness facet behind structure: preference for routine, planning, tidiness, and rule-following.

### Structure
- Predicts organization and reliability more than raw output
- Very high orderliness shades into rigidity and perfectionism
- Separable from Industriousness — a messy desk and relentless follow-through can coexist

### Sources
- DeYoung, Quilty & Peterson (2007). Between facets and domains.

## node: enthusiasm
title: Enthusiasm
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Facet

The Extraversion facet of warmth and positive affect: sociability, friendliness, and easily activated joy.

### Structure
- The facet most tied to experienced positive emotion
- Predicts social connection and approachability
- Distinct from Assertiveness — warmth without dominance is common

### Sources
- DeYoung, Quilty & Peterson (2007). Between facets and domains.

## node: assertiveness
title: Assertiveness
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Facet

The Extraversion facet of agency and drive: taking charge, speaking up, and pushing toward goals.

### Structure
- Predicts leadership emergence and social influence
- Can read as dominance when Agreeableness is low
- Distinct from Enthusiasm — drive without warmth is common

### Sources
- DeYoung, Quilty & Peterson (2007). Between facets and domains.

## node: compassion
title: Compassion
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Facet

The Agreeableness facet of emotional concern: feeling and acting on others' wellbeing.

### Structure
- The "care" half of Agreeableness — empathy, tenderness, helping
- Predicts prosocial behavior more strongly than Politeness
- Distinct from norm-respect: one can care deeply and still break rules for people

### Sources
- DeYoung, Quilty & Peterson (2007). Between facets and domains.

## node: politeness
title: Politeness
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Facet

The Agreeableness facet of norm-respect: deference, courtesy, and avoidance of confrontation.

### Structure
- The "restraint" half of Agreeableness — suppressing aggressive and exploitative impulses
- Low politeness predicts confrontational and antagonistic styles
- Separable from Compassion: rule-following without warmth is common

### Sources
- DeYoung, Quilty & Peterson (2007). Between facets and domains.

## node: withdrawal
title: Withdrawal
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Facet

The Neuroticism facet of anxious avoidance: worry, risk-aversion, and pulling back from potential threat.

### Structure
- The anticipatory side of negative emotion — distress before the event
- Predicts avoidance behaviors and rumination
- Pairs with Volatility under the Neuroticism trait

### Bridges
- [er-situation-selection] Chronic avoidance is situation selection running on threat default.

### Sources
- DeYoung, Quilty & Peterson (2007). Between facets and domains.

## node: volatility
title: Volatility
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Hierarchy / Facet

The Neuroticism facet of reactive negative emotion: irritability, mood swings, and being easily provoked.

### Structure
- The reactive side of negative emotion — distress in the event
- Predicts anger and emotional lability under stress
- High volatility makes late-stage emotion regulation strategies especially costly

### Bridges
- [suppression] Volatile responders lean on suppression — the least effective strategy.

### Sources
- DeYoung, Quilty & Peterson (2007). Between facets and domains.
