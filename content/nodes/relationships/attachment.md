## node: attachment
title: Adult Attachment Theory
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Field / 2D plane

Adult attachment is best understood as a two-dimensional field: anxiety (fear of abandonment) and avoidance (discomfort with closeness) are continuous axes. The familiar four "styles" are region labels on this plane, not boxes people live in.

### Structure
- Anxiety axis: from comfortable trust to hyperactivating preoccupation with the relationship
- Avoidance axis: from comfortable interdependence to deactivating self-reliance
- Every position on the plane is a coordinate, not a category — most people sit between regions
- Strong meta-analytic support; the dimensional model (Brennan/Fraley) outperforms categorical typing psychometrically

### Note
Most users sit between regions. Styles are helpful labels, but the underlying model is dimensional — and position on the field can shift with insight, therapy, and experience of secure relationships.

### Bridges
- [attachment-anxiety-axis] The abandonment-vigilance dimension.
- [attachment-avoidance-axis] The closeness-discomfort dimension.
- [bowlby] Where the framework comes from — the developmental origin story.
- [neuroticism] Trait-level anxiety amplifies attachment anxiety.
- [sdt-relatedness] Secure attachment satisfies the relatedness need; insecurity starves it.
- [emotion-regulation] Attachment pattern predicts regulation strategy — hyperactivation and suppression map onto the two axes.
- [self-compassion] Secure attachment correlates with self-compassion; each can bootstrap the other.

### Sources
- Brennan, Clark & Shaver (1998). Self-report measurement of adult attachment.
- Fraley & Waller (1998). Adult attachment patterns: a test of the typological model.
- Mikulincer & Shaver (2016). Attachment in Adulthood.

## node: attachment-anxiety-axis
title: Anxiety axis
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Field / Axis

The first dimension of the attachment plane: how vigilantly the mind monitors for abandonment.

### Structure
- Low: comfortable trusting; ambiguity reads as neutral
- High: hyperactivating strategies — amplified bids for contact, reassurance-seeking, preoccupation with the relationship's security
- Continuous, not categorical — everyone has a position on this axis

### Bridges
- [neuroticism] Attachment anxiety and trait anxiety share variance.

### Sources
- Brennan, Clark & Shaver (1998). Self-report measurement of adult attachment.

## node: attachment-avoidance-axis
title: Avoidance axis
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Field / Axis

The second dimension of the attachment plane: how comfortable the mind is with closeness and dependence.

### Structure
- Low: comfortable with intimacy and interdependence
- High: deactivating strategies — minimizing needs, maintaining distance, preferring self-reliance
- Often an adaptation to early environments that rewarded self-sufficiency

### Bridges
- [suppression] Deactivation leans on expressive suppression — with its documented costs.

### Sources
- Brennan, Clark & Shaver (1998). Self-report measurement of adult attachment.

## node: attachment-secure
title: Secure
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Field / Region

Low anxiety, low avoidance. Comfortable with both closeness and independence.

### Structure
- Communicates needs directly; tolerates partner autonomy without threat
- In conflict: engages, repairs, assumes goodwill
- Under stress: seeks support and uses it effectively
- Most predictive of relationship satisfaction and adaptive emotion regulation

### Note
Security is a region tendency, not a personality award — secure-leaning people still have insecure moments.

### Bridges
- [self-compassion] Security and self-compassion correlate — an internalized caregiver in both cases.

### Sources
- Mikulincer & Shaver (2016). Attachment in Adulthood.

## node: attachment-anxious
title: Anxious / Preoccupied
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Field / Region

High anxiety, low avoidance. Craves closeness but fears abandonment.

### Structure
- Hyperactivates the attachment system — amplified bids for contact and reassurance
- In conflict: protest behaviors, pursuit, difficulty disengaging
- Under stress: support-seeking that can overwhelm the support
- Reads ambiguity as threat; linked to higher reactivity and lower use of reappraisal

### Note
Patterns can shift with insight, therapy, and the lived experience of secure relationships.

### Bridges
- [er-attentional-deployment] Rumination is hyperactivation running on attention.
- [reappraisal] The underused strategy in this region — and the trainable one.

### Sources
- Mikulincer & Shaver (2016). Attachment in Adulthood.

## node: attachment-dismissing
title: Dismissing-Avoidant
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Field / Region

Low anxiety, high avoidance. Values self-reliance; deactivates attachment needs.

### Structure
- Discomfort with emotional intimacy and disclosure
- In conflict: withdraws, stonewalls, changes the subject
- Under stress: goes it alone; support feels like exposure
- Often presents as confident independence — the cost is depth of connection

### Note
Avoidance is usually an adaptation to early environments that rewarded self-sufficiency, not an absence of attachment needs.

### Bridges
- [suppression] The signature strategy of deactivation — hiding expression while arousal continues.

### Sources
- Mikulincer & Shaver (2016). Attachment in Adulthood.

## node: attachment-fearful
title: Fearful-Avoidant
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Field / Region

High anxiety, high avoidance. Wants closeness and fears it simultaneously.

### Structure
- Oscillates between seeking and withdrawing — the pull and the push
- In conflict: escalation followed by shutdown
- Under stress: the two strategies collide; behavior looks disorganized
- Understanding the two competing drives is more useful than the label

### Note
The "disorganized" label has thinner measurement support in adults than the two underlying dimensions — another reason to think in axes, not types.

### Sources
- Mikulincer & Shaver (2016). Attachment in Adulthood.
