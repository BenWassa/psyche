## node: emotion-regulation
title: Emotion Regulation Process Model
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Timeline / Strategies

Emotion unfolds over time — situation, attention, appraisal, response — and can be regulated at five points along that timeline. Where you intervene matters more than how hard you try.

### Structure
- Five intervention points, ordered: situation selection → situation modification → attentional deployment → cognitive change (reappraisal) → response modulation
- The key finding: earlier interventions systematically outperform later ones
- Reappraisal is the best-supported strategy; suppression the least effective, with physiological, cognitive, and relational costs
- Gross's model — the dominant framework in emotion-regulation research

### Bridges
- [er-situation-selection] Choose the situation before the emotion begins.
- [er-situation-modification] Reshape the situation you're in.
- [er-attentional-deployment] Steer attention within the situation.
- [reappraisal] Reinterpret what the situation means.
- [suppression] The late-stage strategy — and why it backfires.
- [neuroticism] High trait Neuroticism predicts reliance on late, maladaptive strategies.
- [attachment] Attachment pattern predicts strategy: hyperactivation vs deactivation.

### Sources
- Gross (1998). The emerging field of emotion regulation.
- Webb, Miles & Sheeran (2012). Dealing with feeling (meta-analysis of ER strategies).

## node: er-situation-selection
title: Situation Selection
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Timeline / Stage 1

Choose which situations to enter or avoid based on their likely emotional impact — the earliest and often the most effective intervention point.

### Structure
- Requires knowing your own emotional patterns before the situation begins
- Examples: declining the dinner with a hostile colleague; scheduling hard conversations for peak energy
- The failure mode is chronic avoidance — selection in service of growth, not escape

### Bridges
- [withdrawal] When threat-default runs selection, it becomes avoidance.

### Sources
- Gross (2015). Emotion regulation: current status and future prospects.

## node: er-situation-modification
title: Situation Modification
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Timeline / Stage 2

Change the situation itself to alter its emotional trajectory — you stay in it, but reshape it.

### Structure
- More flexible than avoidance; requires some agency over conditions
- Examples: moving a difficult conversation to a private setting; breaking an overwhelming task into pieces
- A motivational act as much as an emotional one — this is where regulation meets goal pursuit

### Bridges
- [goal-setting] Modification is goal-setting machinery applied to emotional stakes.

### Sources
- Gross (2015). Emotion regulation: current status and future prospects.

## node: er-attentional-deployment
title: Attentional Deployment
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Timeline / Stage 3

Direct attention within the situation — toward or away from emotionally charged elements.

### Structure
- Distraction: effective short-term for mild negative emotion
- Concentration: sustained focus on neutral or workable aspects
- Rumination is this strategy gone wrong — sustained focus on negative content amplifies distress

### Bridges
- [attachment-anxious] Rumination and reassurance-scanning are attentional deployment under hyperactivation.

### Sources
- Gross (2015). Emotion regulation: current status and future prospects.

## node: reappraisal
title: Cognitive Reappraisal
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Timeline / Stage 4

Change how you interpret the situation and the emotion changes with it — the most empirically supported regulation strategy.

### Structure
- Intervenes before the emotional response consolidates
- Lower physiological and relational cost than suppression
- Examples: nerves as excitement; a hard conversation as a relationship investment
- Trainable — the skill improves with deliberate practice

### Note
Reappraisal requires deliberate System 2 engagement, and it is the formal target of CBT's cognitive restructuring — the two literatures describe the same mechanism.

### Bridges
- [appraisal-secondary] What reappraisal actually changes: the coping appraisal.
- [cbt-restructuring] The clinical protocol for the same move.
- [dual-process-system2] The deliberate system doing the reinterpreting.

### Sources
- Gross & John (2003). Individual differences in two emotion regulation processes.

## node: suppression
title: Suppression
tier: cornerstone
grammar: Timeline / Stage 5

Inhibiting the outward expression of emotion after it has emerged — the least effective strategy, with documented costs.

### Structure
- Physiological: arousal continues internally despite the hidden expression
- Cognitive: increases load and impairs memory of the interaction
- Relational: partners of suppressors report feeling less close and less understood

### Note
Suppression is not composure. Choosing when and how to express is regulation; blanket inhibition is the strategy that backfires.

### Bridges
- [attachment-dismissing] The signature strategy of attachment deactivation.
- [neuroticism] High-N responders default here most — and pay the most for it.

### Sources
- Gross & John (2003). Individual differences in two emotion regulation processes.
