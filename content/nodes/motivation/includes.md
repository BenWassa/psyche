## node: regulatory-focus
title: Regulatory Focus Theory
tier: include
grammar: Single dimension

People pursue goals with a promotion orientation (growth, gains, aspirations) or a prevention orientation (security, obligations, loss-avoidance) — a single bipolar dimension with distinct strategies at each pole.

### Structure
- Promotion: eagerness strategies, exploration, higher risk tolerance; failing feels like dejection
- Prevention: vigilance strategies, caution, thoroughness; failing feels like agitation
- Regulatory fit: performance and satisfaction improve when goal framing matches orientation
- Most people use both orientations contextually — a tendency, not a type

### Note
The best entry point for "how do I reframe my goals": recast the same goal in promotion terms (what I gain) or prevention terms (what I protect) and notice which one moves you.

### Bridges
- [appraisal] Promotion vs prevention shapes whether demands read as challenge or threat.
- [goal-setting] A framing layer on top of the goal-performance loop.

### Sources
- Higgins (1997). Beyond pleasure and pain.

## node: expectancy-theory
title: Expectancy Theory
tier: include
grammar: Formula

Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence. Multiplicative — if any factor is zero, motivation is zero. Use it as a diagnostic for where motivation breaks down.

### Structure
- Expectancy: "Can my effort produce good performance?" — a self-efficacy question
- Instrumentality: "Will good performance actually lead to outcomes?"
- Valence: "Do I value those outcomes?"
- Because the terms multiply, the weakest factor is the binding constraint

### Note
When motivation fails, identify which factor is closest to zero — that is the lever to work on. Effort-confidence problems, broken reward links, and hollow rewards need different fixes.

### Bridges
- [self-efficacy] Expectancy is the efficacy judgment wearing a different name.
- [sdt-continuum] Valence connects to where the outcome sits on your value continuum.

### Sources
- Vroom (1964). Work and Motivation.
