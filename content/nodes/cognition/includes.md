## node: cognitive-dissonance
title: Cognitive Dissonance
tier: include
grammar: Process loop

Holding two conflicting cognitions creates psychological discomfort — usually resolved by rationalizing rather than by changing the belief or the behavior.

### Structure
- Three resolution paths: change a belief (rare), change a behavior (sometimes), rationalize (System 1's preferred route)
- Everyday forms: self-justification, selective attention to confirming evidence, effort justification ("I worked hard, so it must be worth it")
- The tension-then-resolution shape makes this a process loop, not a static claim

### Note
The construct survives; the classic paradigm did not. Vaidis et al. (2024), a 39-lab replication, failed to support the 1959 induced-compliance design. Teach the phenomenon — rationalization is everyday-observable — not the original study.

### Bridges
- [confirmation-bias] Dissonance is often resolved through confirmation rather than revision.
- [attribution-theory] Dissonance about others is often resolved by dispositional attribution.
- [self-concept] Self-justification protects the self-image — dissonance frequently involves self-concept threat.

### Sources
- Festinger (1957). A Theory of Cognitive Dissonance.
- Vaidis et al. (2024). A multilab replication of the induced-compliance paradigm.

## node: attribution-theory
title: Attribution Theory & the FAE
tier: include
grammar: Hierarchy / Errors

We explain behavior by attributing it to the person or to the situation — and systematically over-attribute other people's behavior to their disposition.

### Structure
- Fundamental Attribution Error: their lateness is character; mine is traffic
- Self-Serving Bias: my successes are me; my failures are circumstances
- Actor–Observer Asymmetry: my behavior is situational; yours is dispositional
- In-group/Out-group Asymmetry: our failures are situational; theirs are dispositional

### Note
The FAE is robust but its magnitude varies more than the canonical framing suggests: East Asian samples show smaller effects, and Malle's 2006 meta-analysis substantially reduced the actor–observer estimate.

### Bridges
- [dual-process-system1] The FAE is a System 1 heuristic for person-perception.
- [attachment] Insecure attachment amplifies dispositional attribution toward partners.
- [heuristics] Attribution errors belong to the same shortcut family as judgment biases.

### Sources
- Ross (1977). The intuitive psychologist and his shortcomings.
- Malle (2006). The actor–observer asymmetry in attribution (meta-analysis).
