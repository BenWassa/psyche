# Psychology Literacy App — Comprehensive Academic Literature Review

## TL;DR

- **Big Five/OCEAN, Self-Determination Theory, Adult Attachment, Dual-Process/Heuristics, and Self-Efficacy form an empirically robust "cornerstone five"** that should anchor any psychology-literacy app for educated lay adults; each is supported by post-2015 meta-analytic evidence and translates well to everyday decisions, relationships, and motivation.
- **Several widely-known "classics" should be excluded or strongly caveated**: Myers-Briggs (low test-retest reliability, no scientific consensus), strict Maslow hierarchy (sequential ordering rejected by large empirical tests), power posing (behavioural effects do not replicate), ego depletion (effect ≈ 0 in registered replications), Type A→heart disease (largely refuted), and parts of Terror Management Theory (z-curve analyses suggest much of the literature is selection-biased).
- **Recommended structure: a 4-domain MVP (Personality & Self, Motivation & Goals, Relationships & Attachment, Thinking & Judgment) expandable to 6 (adding Emotion & Regulation, Social Identity & Influence)**. Sequencing should start with Personality (gives users a self-map), then Thinking (judgment errors are universal hooks), before progressing to relational and motivational topics that build on those foundations.

---

## Key Findings

1. **Domain organisation is highly convergent.** Major textbooks (Gerrig, Myers, Schacter; APA's curriculum framework) repeatedly use ~6–8 sub-disciplines: biological, cognitive, developmental, social, personality, clinical/abnormal, and (increasingly) emotion/affective and cultural. For a non-clinical lay app, four to six of these domains map directly to "everyday adult life."
2. **Replication crisis has materially shifted the empirical map.** Effects once treated as foundational — ego depletion, power posing, much of social priming, behavioural mortality-salience effects, and stereotype threat outside narrow conditions — have not held up under registered replications. The big *constructs* (e.g., self-control, threat, identity) survive; the *specific paradigm effects* often do not.
3. **Trait taxonomies are the most replicable area of personality.** The Big Five (and to a lesser extent HEXACO) show cross-cultural structural invariance in dozens of countries; Myers-Briggs does not meet psychometric standards used for the Big Five.
4. **Motivation has a clear winner for lay use.** Self-Determination Theory (autonomy, competence, relatedness) has the broadest meta-analytic backing across education, work, health, and ageing. Maslow's strict hierarchy is contradicted by the Tay & Diener (2011) Gallup data and by Mexico-data tests (Villalobos et al., 2023): the *content* of needs is useful, but the pyramid ordering is empirically rejected.
5. **Attachment theory translated to adults is robust enough for a lay app**, particularly the dimensional (anxiety × avoidance) view from Brennan/Fraley over the categorical four-style view; multiple meta-analyses link adult attachment to relationship quality, therapy outcome, mindfulness, and self-compassion.
6. **Cognition/judgment** is anchored by Dual-Process theory (which Kahneman himself has caveated) and the heuristics-and-biases tradition, where anchoring, availability, and base-rate neglect remain robustly replicated, while specific demonstrations from *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (priming effects, ego depletion) do not.
7. **Some classic social-psychology demonstrations need careful framing.** The Milgram and bystander findings exist but are more conditional than the canonical telling: Burger's (2009) partial replication and Fischer et al.'s (2011) meta-analysis show obedience/non-helping are *moderated by danger, ambiguity, and group composition* rather than uniform.
8. **Cross-domain integrators.** Self-Determination Theory, Big Five, attachment, dual-process, and self-efficacy each connect to multiple other theories — these should be the "hub nodes" of the app's information architecture.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

## Section 3 — Replication Crisis Watch List

| Theory / Effect | Current expert consensus | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| **Ego depletion** | Effectively null in two large multi-lab registered replications (Hagger et al. 2016, d=0.04; Vohs et al. 2021, with Bayesian evidence ~4× favouring null). Some advocates argue specific depleting tasks still produce effects, but the strong "willpower as muscle" model is not supported. | **Exclude as scientific claim**; mention only as historical example of replication crisis. |
| **Power posing** | Lead author Carney publicly disavowed in 2016. Special issue of CRSP (Cesario et al. 2017) and follow-ups: behavioural and hormonal effects fail to replicate. Felt power may shift modestly; downstream behaviour does not. | **Exclude** as practical advice; can be referenced as a cautionary tale. |
| **Milgram generalisability** | Burger (2009) found obedience comparable to Milgram in modern US sample, but only to 150V. Haslam & Reicher's reanalyses argue Milgram's data show "engaged followership," not blind obedience. Procedural variations in original studies also produced wide rate variation. | **Caveat / Revise.** Teach as evidence that situations powerfully shape behaviour, with identity and authority legitimacy as moderators — not as proof "65% will kill." |
| **Stereotype threat scope** | Multiple failed replications (Finnigan & Corker 2016; Forscher et al. data); meta-analyses split (Picho-Kiroga et al. 2021 finds small effect; Warne 2021 reanalysis suggests publication-bias-consistent null). Effects strongest with subtle cues among already-identified groups; small in real-world testing settings. | **Caveat.** Frame as a context-dependent, smaller-than-once-claimed effect, not a universal explanation of group test gaps. |
| **Maslow's hierarchy (empirical status)** | Strict sequential pyramid rejected (Tay & Diener 2011 Gallup data, 123 countries; Villalobos et al. 2023 Mexico data). Need *content* is plausible; ordering is not. | **Revise.** Present as a list/menu of human needs, not a ladder. SDT is the more empirically supported successor. |
| **Myers-Briggs (validity)** | Test-retest reliability poor; dichotomous categories not empirically supported; widely regarded as pseudoscience by mainstream personality psychology. Continuous Big Five overlaps with three of four MBTI dimensions but treats them as continua. | **Exclude as a scientific tool**; address as cultural/popular phenomenon. |
| **Type A personality → heart disease** | Original Western Collaborative Group Study finding; subsequent meta-analyses largely failed to confirm. Hostility component (not the full Type A construct) retains some predictive value. | **Exclude as taught.** Retain "hostility/cynicism associated with cardiovascular risk" only. |
| **Grit as distinct from conscientiousness** | Credé et al. (2017) meta-analysis: grit's perseverance facet ≈ conscientiousness; incremental validity small; consistency facet weak. | **Caveat.** Teach as a popularised facet of conscientiousness, not a separate trait. |
| **Terror Management Theory mortality-salience effects** | 2023 PLOS ONE multi-study failures; Schimmack (2025) z-curve of 800+ studies suggests selection-bias-driven literature; Schindler et al. (2023) meta-analysis with methodological controls shows much weaker effects than original 1.0+ d's. | **Exclude or strong caveat.** Existential concerns are real; the specific mortality-salience paradigm is fragile. |
| **Social priming (incidental, à la Bargh elderly walking)** | Many studies failed to replicate; Kahneman acknowledged the chapter on priming in *Thinking, Fast and Slow* is the part he'd most retract. | **Exclude.** |
| **Implicit Association Test as predictor of individual behaviour** (not on user list but commonly known) | Meta-analyses (Oswald et al. 2013; Forscher et al. 2019) show very small predictive validity for individual behaviour; test-retest reliability mediocre. | **Caveat.** Teach as measure of associations, not of personal prejudice or future action. |
| **Learning styles (VARK etc.)** (not on list, widely-known) | No credible evidence that matching teaching to "style" improves outcomes (Pashler et al. 2008; Newton 2015). | **Exclude.** |
| **Mozart effect / brain-training general transfer** (not on list) | Meta-analyses do not support general cognitive transfer from commercial brain-training (Simons et al. 2016). | **Exclude.** |
| **Losada / Fredrickson positivity ratio (3:1)** | Mathematically refuted (Brown, Sokal & Friedman 2013) and partially retracted. | **Exclude the ratio**; retain Broaden-and-Build core. |
| **Marshmallow test as childhood predictor of adult success** (not on list) | Watts, Duncan & Quan (2018) replication: original effect largely explained by socioeconomic confounds. | **Caveat.** |

## Section 4 — Structural Type Classification

Why structural type matters for UI: each type implies a different visual/interaction metaphor — taxonomies suit profile/spider charts; dimensional theories suit sliders; stage theories suit timelines; process theories suit flow diagrams; situational theories suit branching/scenario UIs.

| Theory | Structural type | UI implication |
|---|---|---|
| Big Five / OCEAN | **Trait/taxonomy + dimensional** | Five sliders/spider chart; user maps self on continuous axes. |
| HEXACO | **Trait/taxonomy + dimensional** | Six-axis radar chart; emphasise added Honesty-Humility dimension. |
| Dark Triad | **Trait/taxonomy** (overlapping dimensions) | Three-axis chart; warn on construct overlap. |
| Self-Determination Theory | **Needs/motivational + dimensional** (autonomous↔controlled continuum) | Three "fuel gauges" for autonomy, competence, relatedness; continuum for motivation type. |
| Maslow (revised) | **Needs taxonomy** (not stage) | Present as horizontal list of need categories, not a pyramid. |
| Goal-Setting Theory | **Process/applied** | Step-by-step goal builder UI; difficulty + specificity sliders. |
| Self-Efficacy | **Dimensional + domain-specific** | Per-task confidence sliders; emphasise domain specificity. |
| Regulatory Focus | **Dimensional** (promotion ↔ prevention) | Two-axis or single bipolar slider; situational priming examples. |
| Adult Attachment (dimensional) | **Dimensional** | 2-D plot: anxiety × avoidance, with quadrant labels for accessibility. |
| Dual-Process Theory | **Process/cognitive** | Decision-flow diagram; show System 1 default/System 2 override. |
| Heuristics & Biases | **Process/cognitive** (catalog) | Card deck of biases, each with example + debiasing tip. |
| Cognitive Dissonance | **Process/motivational** | Tension-resolution loop diagram. |
| Appraisal Theory | **Process** | Stimulus → appraisal → emotion flow diagram. |
| Constructed Emotion | **Process/cognitive** | Predictive-brain visualisation; counter-intuitive teaching format. |
| Broaden-and-Build | **Process** (upward spiral) | Spiral visualisation linking emotion to resource-building. |
| Emotion Regulation Process Model | **Process** (timeline of strategies) | Timeline UI: situation selection → modification → attention → reappraisal → response modulation. |
| Social Identity Theory | **Social/situational** | Scenario UI showing categorisation → identification → comparison. |
| Conformity / Bystander / Obedience | **Social/situational** | Scenario simulator; emphasise moderating conditions. |
| Self-Compassion | **Dimensional + practice-based** | Three-component slider (kindness, common humanity, mindfulness); practice prompts. |
| Erikson's stages (adult) | **Stage/developmental** (loosely sequential) | Lifespan timeline with revisitable stages. |
| CBT Model (Beck) | **Process/cognitive** | Triangle of thoughts–feelings–behaviour with bi-directional arrows. |

## Section 5 — Cross-Domain Connection Map

| Theory A ↔ Theory B | Nature of connection | Why it matters for a lay user |
|---|---|---|
| Big Five (Conscientiousness) ↔ Grit / Goal-Setting / Self-Efficacy | Conscientiousness underlies both grit's perseverance and persistent goal pursuit; self-efficacy contributes to conscientious task completion. | Helps users see that "discipline" is not one mysterious trait but several interacting, modifiable processes. |
| Big Five (Neuroticism) ↔ Emotion Regulation / Appraisal Theory | High neuroticism = more frequent negative appraisals and harder regulation; ER skills can buffer trait neuroticism's effects. | Explains why some people "feel things harder" but can still build skills. |
| Adult Attachment ↔ Self-Compassion ↔ Emotion Regulation | Hill et al. (2025) meta-analysis: secure attachment positively correlated with self-compassion; both predict adaptive ER. | Anchors a coherent self-other care narrative; relationship history → self-relationship → emotional skills. |
| Self-Determination Theory ↔ Self-Efficacy ↔ Goal-Setting | Competence need ≈ self-efficacy; autonomous goals (SDT) plus specific/difficult targets (Locke) → optimal performance & well-being. | Gives users a unified motivation toolkit rather than competing prescriptions. |
| Dual-Process Theory ↔ Heuristics & Biases ↔ Cognitive Dissonance | System 1 generates heuristic judgments; dissonance is felt when System 2 detects inconsistency; debiasing requires System 2 engagement. | Explains *why* we have biases and how rationalisation patches them post hoc. |
| Appraisal Theory ↔ CBT Model ↔ Constructed Emotion | All argue: situations are interpreted, not directly experienced; reinterpretation changes emotion. | Single underlying lever — reappraisal — explains diverse therapeutic and self-help techniques. |
| Social Identity Theory ↔ Conformity ↔ Attribution Theory | In-group identification shapes whom we conform to and how we attribute behaviour ("us" gets situational charity, "them" gets dispositional blame). | Reframes "tribalism" mechanistically. |
| Bystander Effect ↔ Social Identity Theory ↔ Diffusion of Responsibility | Shared in-group membership with victim attenuates bystander effect; identity moderates diffusion. | Practical insight: "be specific, name a person" works because it breaks anonymity/diffusion. |
| Self-Efficacy ↔ Self-Determination Theory ↔ Goal-Setting | Competence (SDT) is conceptual sibling of self-efficacy (Bandura); both feed sustained goal pursuit. | Allows the app to introduce one unified concept rather than three near-synonyms. |
| Big Five (Agreeableness/Honesty-Humility) ↔ Dark Triad | Dark Triad ≈ low Agreeableness + low Honesty-Humility + (varying) low Conscientiousness. | Demystifies "dark personalities" as low ends of normal traits, not separate species. |
| Cognitive Dissonance ↔ Self-Concept ↔ Attribution Theory | Self-serving attribution and self-justification both reduce dissonance and protect self-concept. | Explains everyday rationalisation ("I'm a good person, so this can't have been wrong"). |
| Erikson (intimacy) ↔ Adult Attachment ↔ Self-Compassion | Achieving "intimacy" in Erikson's sense rests on attachment security and capacity for vulnerable self-acceptance. | Connects developmental framing to actionable relationship skills. |
| Broaden-and-Build ↔ SDT ↔ Self-Compassion | Positive emotions broaden cognition and build resources; SDT need-satisfaction and self-compassion are reliable positive-emotion generators. | Gives users a virtuous-cycle model for well-being. |
| Social Comparison ↔ Self-Concept ↔ Social Identity | Comparisons are central to self-evaluation and intensify within identity-relevant in-groups. | Critical for navigating social-media age. |
| Heuristics & Biases ↔ Attribution Theory ↔ Dual-Process | Fundamental attribution error is itself a System 1 heuristic; correspondence bias is heuristic person-perception. | Connects "thinking errors" to "people-judgment errors." |

## Section 6 — Recommended Domain Set

### 4-Domain MVP

| # | Domain | Cornerstone theories | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Personality & Self-Understanding** | Big Five (with HEXACO option), Self-Efficacy, Self-Compassion | Highest immediate self-relevance; entry point that gives users a personalised map. Anchored by the most replicated structure in psychology. |
| 2 | **Motivation & Goals** | Self-Determination Theory, Goal-Setting, (revised) Maslow as need taxonomy | Universal lay relevance ("how do I get myself to do things"); strongest applied evidence base. |
| 3 | **Relationships & Attachment** | Bowlby, Adult Attachment (dimensional), Self-Compassion, Social Identity Theory (light) | Covers the relational half of life with empirically robust theories that translate naturally to romantic, family, and work relationships. |
| 4 | **Thinking & Judgment** | Dual-Process Theory, Heuristics & Biases, Cognitive Dissonance, Attribution / Fundamental Attribution Error | The "rationality literacy" pillar; high engagement and direct everyday utility. |

**Why these four:** they cover the four questions in the brief — self-understanding, relationships, motivation, and thinking clearly — with the *highest-replicating* theories in each space. Each can stand alone but cross-references the others (e.g., conscientiousness in Personality links forward to Goal-Setting; attachment in Relationships links back to Self-Compassion in Personality).

### Notes on Sequencing & Dependencies

- **Recommended onboarding sequence:** Personality → Thinking & Judgment → Motivation & Goals → Relationships & Attachment → Emotion & Regulation → Social Identity. Personality first gives users a self-map and a "trait vocabulary" they will reuse. Thinking next because biases are universally engaging hooks and require no other content. Motivation builds on Self-Efficacy from Personality. Relationships builds on Self-Compassion. Emotion synthesises Cognition + Self. Social Identity is the most context-heavy and benefits from earlier vocabulary.
- **Dependency map**: 
  - Goal-Setting depends on Self-Efficacy (introduce in Personality module).
  - Adult Attachment (dimensional version) is best introduced after a continuous-trait mindset has been established in Personality.
  - Cognitive Dissonance and Attribution work best after Dual-Process is taught, because they're easiest to explain as System 1 outputs.
  - Emotion Regulation (Gross) presupposes Appraisal Theory.
  - Social Identity Theory benefits from prior introduction of attribution and biases.
- **Cross-domain "hub" content**: build short shared modules for (a) Conscientiousness/Self-Control (links Personality, Motivation, Goals, Grit caveat), (b) Reappraisal (links Cognition, Emotion, CBT), and (c) Need Satisfaction (links SDT, Maslow critique, Self-Compassion, Attachment).
- **Quality gates for any included content**: post-2015 meta-analytic support OR pre-registered replication; if a finding fails this test, present it only with an explicit "evidence status" caveat (Strong / Moderate / Weak / Contested).

## Caveats

1. **Active research areas.** Constructed Emotion vs. Basic Emotions, the precise scope of stereotype threat, and the status of cognitive dissonance's induced-compliance paradigm are *unsettled* in the current literature. Where the app teaches these, it should explicitly flag the live debate rather than pick a winner.
2. **Cross-cultural generalisation is uneven.** The Big Five replicates broadly but is weaker in small-scale societies (e.g., Tsimane data); SDT need universality is supported but with cultural moderation; Maslow's ordering fails outside Western samples; conformity/obedience/in-group bias all show culture-dependent magnitudes. Any app that uses examples should not present Western-sample findings as universal.
3. **Effect sizes are usually small to moderate.** Even robust findings in personality–outcome and motivation–outcome research typically explain 4–25% of variance. The app should resist the pop-psychology temptation to imply that any single trait or intervention is decisive.
4. **Categorical labels (attachment styles, MBTI types, Dark Triad "types") are pedagogically attractive but psychometrically weaker** than the underlying continuous dimensions. Where the app uses them for accessibility, it should make the dimensional reality visible (e.g., "you are 70% toward the anxious end").
5. **Replication-crisis evidence is itself evolving.** Some "casualties" (e.g., ego depletion) have advocates arguing for boundary conditions; some "survivors" (e.g., anchoring) have specific instantiations that have failed to replicate. The watch-list above reflects the post-2015 consensus but should be revisited every 12–18 months.
6. **Source-quality note.** Several search results in compiling this review came from secondary outlets (Simply Psychology, Positive Psychology, popular blogs). Where they were used, they were corroborated against peer-reviewed meta-analyses, registered replications (e.g., Hagger et al. 2016; Vaidis et al. 2024; Klein et al. 2022 ManyLabs), or primary articles in journals such as *Psychological Bulletin*, *Psychological Science*, *PLOS ONE*, *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, and the *Annual Review of Psychology*. Pop-psychology sources alone were not used as evidence for empirical claims.
7. **Grit, Type A, and MBTI** retain large lay followings and are likely to be searched for. The app should address them directly — explaining *why* they don't pass scientific scrutiny — rather than ignoring them, since users will encounter them elsewhere.
8. **The "Cornerstone/Include/Defer/Exclude" verdicts are recommendations for a non-clinical lay-app context.** Several "Defer" items (Vygotsky, Piaget, Vroom) remain canonical in academic curricula and clinical/educational training, but offer comparatively low marginal value to an educated adult navigating self-understanding, relationships, motivation, and clear thinking.
